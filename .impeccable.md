# isthisaromanroad.com

Design context for this project. Every design skill (audit, critique, polish, normalize, animate, etc.) reads from here. Update this file when the brief, audience, or aesthetic intent changes — never let it lie about the codebase.

## Design Context

### Users

UK drivers and the curious kids in their back seat. The canonical user moment is a parent at a junction in a country lane, phone in one hand, child in the seat behind asking "is this a Roman road?". They want one number: yes or no. If no, how far to the nearest, and which way.

Secondary: history nerds, walkers, school kids learning Roman Britain, anyone who watched *The Eagle* and now keeps spotting straight A-roads.

The job is **a single tap, a single answer, sometimes with a moment of historical context.** Not a research tool. Not a route planner. Not a map app.

### Brand Personality

Atmospheric. Historical. Warm. Mysterious. Joyful but professional. The interface should read as a **small artifact** — something handmade, slightly old, deliberate — not enterprise GIS software and not generic SaaS.

Three-word personality: **oracle, vellum, hush.**

Emotional goals:
- The cold load asks a question.
- The wait is the magic — not a spinner.
- The answer is the centerpiece. One word does most of the work.
- The map is evidence, not the answer.
- The footer doesn't sell anything.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Editio II — Parchment & Walnut.** Muted Roman material colour grounded in real pigments and real stone. Serif-led typography (Cormorant Garamond display, Inter body). Mobile-first. Single-page. Light mode is the canonical look; dark mode (walnut/lit-bronze) follows the system preference.

Typography rules:
- Display: **Cormorant Garamond** 700 + 500 italic (self-hosted via Fontsource).
- Body: **Inter Variable** (self-hosted).
- Roman names (Watling Street, Sorviodunum, etc.) are always *italic small caps with a thin gold underline*. This is the most important typographic convention in the project.
- Old-style numerals (`onum`) on coordinates, edition marks, and anywhere figures sit next to serif type.

References that capture the right feel:
- Museum and heritage sites (V&A, British Museum, Pelagios) — editorial type, restrained palette, scholarly tone.

Explicit anti-references — this should never look like:
- **Google Maps / enterprise GIS dashboards.** No saturated tile colours, no layered controls, no panel chrome, no data-viewer aesthetic.
- **2024 AI landing pages.** No cyan-on-dark, no purple→blue gradients, no glassmorphism, no generic icon-card grids, no rounded-rect drop shadows, no neon accents, no dark-mode-default with glowing accents.

### Design Principles

1. **The answer is the page.** Title, gold rule, one word ("Yes." / "Probably not." / "Out of reach."). Everything else subordinates to it.

2. **One question at a time.** Cold load = wordmark + rule + one CTA. Search is a tertiary text link, not a competing input. After an answer the previous question shrinks into the masthead; the new view is the new content.

3. **Atmosphere over chrome.** A map is a sketch on parchment, not a tile grid. Citation/bibliography hide in a `<details>`. Buttons are typographic pills, not material-design slabs. The footer is two characters and a URL.

4. **Italic Latin everywhere.** Place names, road names, the eyebrow on the share card, the italic Latin in the body — applied consistently this single convention does more for "feels Roman" than any colour.

5. **Restraint with one moment of warmth.** The gold mile-marker rule under each heading, the verdigris of the "you're here" pin, the way the Roman road draws itself onto the parchment. Decorative, but used sparingly enough that each instance carries meaning.

### Accessibility

- **WCAG AA is the floor**, AAA where the palette allows it cheaply. Verify contrast on every change. Document any AA-edge pair in the relevant component's review.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` is honoured at the global level (motion duration collapsed to 0.001ms). The Listening state has an explicit override so it doesn't get stuck at low opacity.
- Every interactive element has a ≥44px tap target and a visible focus ring (3px gold outline with 3px offset).
- Light- and dark-mode palettes are both AA-verified; never optimise the dark variant just for "looks cool" without re-running contrast.
- Native disclosure (`<details>`) is preferred over JS modals. Modals are lazy.

### Out of scope for the design language

- Hero metrics, big-number layouts, supporting-stats grids.
- Card grids of any kind. The page has one card-shaped element today (the answer block) and it's earning its keep.
- Bouncy or elastic easing. Real objects decelerate; so do these animations (`easeOutCubic` / `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)`).
- Glassmorphism, drop-shadow ornament, sparklines as decoration.
- Stock photography. The Open Graph card is typographic.
